The Széchenyi Chain Bridge The Széchenyi Chain Bridge, often simply called the Chain Bridge, is the oldest and most famous bridge in Budapest, Hungary. It was built in 1849 and designed by English engineer William Tierney Clark, who was also responsible for the Marlow Bridge in London. The on-site construction was overseen by Scottish engineer Adam Clark. The bridge is a suspension bridge with stone towers and distinctive lion statues at each end, and it was named after István Széchenyi, a 19th-century Hungarian reformer who played a crucial role in its construction. The bridge was originally built using iron elements, but these were replaced with riveted steel in 1914-1915. The original sidewalk railings were also replaced with cast iron/steel in 1949, replicating the original design. The bridge features a sculpture of the Kossuth Coat of Arms at the crown of the tower arches, which is a variation of the Hungarian coat of arms that does not include the Holy Crown on top. During the Siege of Budapest in World War II, the bridge sustained significant damage and was rebuilt in 1949. The rebuilt bridge reused the original towers and replicated the original design to a great extent, including the eyebar chain design and use of pony stiffening trusses. The Széchenyi Chain Bridge was the first permanent bridge over the Danube River in Hungary and marked a significant milestone in the country's transportation history. It is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a popular tourist attraction in Budapest
